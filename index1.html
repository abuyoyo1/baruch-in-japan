🗾 Japan — The Big Picture
General facts about the country you're visiting
🚅
World Record
The Shinkansen has never had a fatal accident in 60 years
Since its launch in 1964 for the Tokyo Olympics, Japan's bullet train network has carried over 10 billion passengers without a single passenger fatality from a crash or derailment. The average delay across the entire network is under 1 minute. You'll be riding it multiple times on this trip — it's as good as it sounds.
🏪
Only in Japan
Japan has more vending machines per capita than any country on Earth
There are roughly 4 million vending machines across Japan — one for every 30 people. They dispense not just drinks but hot meals, umbrellas, fresh eggs, underwear, and even live crabs. In Tokyo you'll pass dozens every day. The machines are immaculately maintained and almost never broken.
🌸
Culture
Japan has a word for "death by overwork" — Karoshi
Japanese work culture is so intense that the government had to legally recognize karoshi (過労死) — death caused by overwork — as a medical condition. Yet the same culture produces the world's most reliable public transport, spotlessly clean cities, and cuisine with more Michelin stars than any country on Earth (including France). A nation of fascinating contradictions.
🗼
History
Tokyo was nearly destroyed twice in 50 years — and rebuilt both times
The Great Kanto Earthquake of 1923 killed over 100,000 people and leveled much of the city. Then WWII firebombing in 1945 destroyed 50% of Tokyo again. The city you'll visit is almost entirely built after 1945 — which makes its seamless blend of tradition and modernity even more remarkable. Ancient shrines stand between 60-story towers because they were deliberately preserved.
🍣
Food
Tokyo has more Michelin stars than any city in the world
With over 200 Michelin-starred restaurants, Tokyo has more than Paris and New York combined. But the real secret is that Japan's food culture runs equally deep at street level — a ¥600 bowl of ramen from a 12-seat shop can be as extraordinary as a ¥30,000 kaiseki dinner. You'll experience both ends of that spectrum on this trip.
⛩️
Culture
Japan has 6,852 islands — and nearly 1,500 earthquakes a year
Japan sits on the "Ring of Fire" at the junction of four tectonic plates, making it one of the most seismically active places on Earth. Buildings are engineered to sway rather than crack — skyscrapers in Tokyo are built on giant shock absorbers. This volcanic geology also gives Japan its extraordinary hot springs (onsen) culture, which you'll taste in Takayama.
Jul 11 — Departure from Tel Aviv
✈️ TLV → DXB → NRT
✈️
History
Israel and Japan established diplomatic ties in 1952 — and share a surprising connection
Both nations were devastated in the mid-20th century and rebuilt from near-zero within a generation. Japan's 1950s economic miracle and Israel's rapid development drew comparisons from economists worldwide. Today Japan is Israel's largest trading partner in Asia. The flight you're on tonight connects two of the world's most technologically advanced small nations.
🇦🇪
Record
Dubai Airport is the world's busiest international airport
Dubai International handles over 87 million passengers a year, surpassing London Heathrow for international traffic. Terminal 3, where you'll transit, is the largest building in the world by floor area — a single structure covering 1.7 million square metres. Your 2-hour layover barely scratches the surface of this sky city.
🌏
Fun Fact
Japan is closer to Israel than most people realize — but it's still a 9.5-hour flight
Tokyo is approximately 9,200 km from Tel Aviv. For context, that's about the same distance as New York to London… twice. Yet Japan and Israel share remarkably similar attitudes toward innovation, food culture, compulsory military service, and family. Many Israelis feel an immediate cultural resonance when they arrive in Japan — a sense of orderliness, intensity, and collective pride that feels somehow familiar.
Jul 12 — Tokyo (Arrival)
🗼 Narita → Kyobashi
🏙️
History
Tokyo was a fishing village called Edo until 1868
For over 250 years, Tokyo was known as Edo — the seat of the Tokugawa shogunate that ruled Japan while the Emperor remained ceremonially in Kyoto. When Emperor Meiji moved to Edo in 1868 and renamed it Tokyo ("Eastern Capital"), it became Japan's political center. The city you're landing in grew from a small estuary settlement into the world's largest metropolitan area — 37 million people — in under 160 years.
🚆
Record
The N'EX you're taking is one of the world's most punctual trains
The Narita Express (N'EX) runs from the airport to central Tokyo in exactly 60 minutes with essentially no delays. JR East issues formal apology letters if a train is more than 5 minutes late — something that happens fewer than a handful of times per year. Station clocks across Japan are synchronized to the second. Welcome to the world's most time-precise country.
🏨
Culture
Your hotel neighborhood, Kyobashi, was once Tokyo's most elegant shopping district
Kyobashi — literally "Capital Bridge" — was the first bridge built after Edo Castle's construction, and its surrounding area became the merchant heart of old Tokyo. The area sat between the castle and the sea, making it a natural commercial hub. Today it's a quiet business district sandwiched between Ginza and Tokyo Station — an ideal base that most tourists overlook entirely.
Jul 13 — Takayama
⛰️ Tokyo → Takayama via Hida Express
🏯
History
Takayama was so isolated by mountains that it developed its own distinct culture
Surrounded on all sides by the Japanese Alps, Takayama remained cut off from the rest of Japan for centuries. This isolation produced a unique style of architecture, cuisine, sake, and craftsmanship that still sets it apart today. The Sanmachi Suji merchant quarter you'll walk through this afternoon looks almost exactly as it did in the Edo period (1600–1868) — no other Japanese city has preserved its old town this completely.
🏛️
History
Takayama Jinya is the only surviving Edo-period government outpost in Japan
The Takayama Jinya — which you'll visit this afternoon — served as the regional government office under the Tokugawa shogunate from 1692. After the Meiji Restoration it continued as a government building until 1969, making it functionally active for nearly 300 years. It's the last remaining example of a "jinya" (regional government outpost) in the entire country. The interrogation room and torture chamber in the basement are… sobering.
🍶
Food
Takayama sake is considered some of Japan's finest — because of its water
The Japanese Alps filter snowmelt through granite and limestone for decades before it emerges as the ultra-pure spring water that feeds Takayama's breweries. This water — soft, mineral-light, and cold — is the secret behind the region's exceptional sake. The brewery district you'll explore this evening has been producing sake in the same family-run workshops since the 1600s. When you see the cedar ball (sugidama) hanging above a door, that's the sign of a brewery.
Jul 14 — Kamikochi
🏔️ Alpine day trip from Takayama
🚗
Only in Japan
Private cars have been banned from Kamikochi since 1975
To protect the pristine alpine environment, Kamikochi is completely off-limits to private vehicles. All visitors arrive by bus or taxi from the Sawando parking area. This means the valley — despite receiving 1.5 million visitors a year — remains remarkably peaceful and unspoiled. The ban was pioneering for its time and is now studied by environmental planners worldwide as a model for protecting fragile natural sites.
🌋
Nature
The Azusa River flowing past Kappa-bashi is fed by an active volcano
The crystal-clear Azusa River originates from the slopes of Mount Yake, one of Japan's few currently active volcanoes — which last erupted in 2008. The volcanic heat warms the groundwater, contributing to the river's extraordinary clarity and the unique ecosystem of the valley. Taisho Pond, the hauntingly beautiful lake you'll visit in the afternoon, was itself created by a volcanic eruption of Mount Yake in 1915, which dammed the river.
🧗
History
Kamikochi was "discovered" by a British missionary in 1891
The Reverend Walter Weston arrived in Kamikochi in 1891 and was so captivated that he wrote a book — "Mountaineering and Exploration in the Japanese Alps" — that introduced the region to the Western world and triggered Japan's mountaineering boom. He's considered the father of Japanese alpinism. A bronze relief of Weston is mounted on a rock near Kappa-bashi; locals hold an annual festival in his honor every June. Look for it as you cross the bridge.
Jul 15 — Shirakawa-go → Kanazawa
🛖 UNESCO village → Sea of Japan coast
🏠
History
Shirakawa-go's roofs are held together without nails — and rebuilt by the whole village
The iconic gassho-zukuri ("hands in prayer") thatched farmhouses are engineered with steep 60-degree roofs to shed the region's massive snowfall — up to 2 metres deep in winter. The roofs are constructed using only rope and wooden pegs, no nails, which allows the structure to flex under snow load without cracking. When a roof needs replacing (every 30–40 years), the entire village gathers in a tradition called yui — cooperative labor. A single roof replacement involves 200+ people working for a single day.
🥷
History
Shirakawa-go may have been a ninja training ground
One persistent local legend holds that the remote valley was used by the Shirakawa clan to secretly train ninja in the feudal period. The extreme isolation, harsh winters, and mountain terrain made it ideal. Whether true or not, Kanazawa — your destination today — has a documented ninja history: the Ninja Temple (Myoryuji) you'll visit on Jul 16 was built with hidden rooms, trapdoors, and escape tunnels specifically to protect the feudal lord from assassination.
🥇
Record
Kanazawa produces 99% of all gold leaf made in Japan
Kanazawa's gold leaf industry dates back over 400 years to the Maeda clan, who encouraged artisans to settle here because gold leaf production was banned everywhere else in Japan (to prevent competition with Kyoto and Edo). Today, a single gram of gold is beaten into sheets so thin they cover 1 square metre. The gold leaf you'll see dusted on ice cream, lacquerware, and temple decorations throughout the city is almost certainly made within a few kilometres of where you're standing.
Jul 16 — Kanazawa → Kyoto
🎏 Arriving for Gion Matsuri Yoiyama
🏯
History
Kyoto was Japan's capital for over 1,000 years — and was almost nuked in 1945
Kyoto served as Japan's imperial capital from 794 to 1868 — over a millennium of continuous rule. In WWII, it was originally first on the US atomic bomb target list because of its cultural and industrial significance. US Secretary of War Henry Stimson personally removed it from the list, having visited Kyoto on his honeymoon and understanding its irreplaceable cultural value. Hiroshima and Nagasaki were bombed instead. The Kyoto you're visiting exists partly because of one man's personal memory of a honeymoon.
🎏
History
The Gion Matsuri started as a plague-prevention ritual in 869 AD
The festival you're arriving for tonight began 1,157 years ago during a catastrophic epidemic, when the people of Kyoto constructed 66 ceremonial halberds — one for each of Japan's then-provinces — and paraded them through the city to appease the gods and end the disease. It worked (or the plague passed), and the tradition has continued almost every year since, making it one of the oldest continuously running festivals on Earth. The giant floats (yamahoko) you'll see tonight weigh up to 12 tonnes and are assembled without a single nail.
🥋
History
The Ninja Temple in Kanazawa has 29 staircases and 7 levels — but looks like a 2-story house
Myoryuji Temple (which you visited this morning) was built in 1643 for the Maeda clan as a surveillance post and escape route. From the outside it appears to be an ordinary 2-story temple. Inside: 29 staircases, 23 rooms across 7 floors, hidden corridors, trapdoor rooms, a well that connects to an underground escape tunnel, and a pit trap in the floor for assassins. The name "Ninja Temple" is informal — actual ninja never lived here. The complexity itself was the defense.
Jul 17 — Kyoto (Grand Parade + Arashiyama)
🏯 Yamahoko Parade + Bamboo Grove
🎋
Nature
The Arashiyama bamboo you'll walk through grows 90cm per day
Moso bamboo — the species growing in the Arashiyama grove — is the fastest-growing plant on Earth, capable of shooting up 90cm (nearly a metre) in a single day during its growth season. The grove's extraordinary acoustic quality — the sound of thousands of hollow bamboo stalks clicking and rustling in the wind — was designated one of Japan's "100 Soundscapes to Preserve." Go early: before 8am the grove is near-empty and deeply atmospheric.
🐒
Nature
The Arashiyama Monkey Park monkeys have their own social hierarchy — and remember human faces
The 120 Japanese macaques (snow monkeys) at Iwatayama Monkey Park live in a complex social structure with a clearly defined alpha and ranked subordinates. Researchers have documented that the monkeys recognize and remember individual human faces for years, and adjust their behavior based on past interactions. They're also one of the only non-human primates known to wash food before eating — a behavior first observed in a Japanese monkey troop in 1953 and now considered a cultural tradition passed between generations.
🛕
Culture
Geisha in Kyoto are called Geiko — and they train for years before their first performance
In Kyoto, geisha are called geiko ("woman of art") rather than geisha — the Tokyo term. Apprentices, called maiko, begin training as teenagers and spend 5+ years mastering dance, music, tea ceremony, flower arrangement, and the art of conversation before their debut. The Gion district you'll walk through tonight is one of only a few remaining hanamachi (flower towns) in Japan. Spot a maiko by her longer hanging obi sash, elaborate hair ornaments, and white makeup — geiko wear simpler styling.
Jul 18 — Kyoto (Culture Day)
🍵 Fushimi Inari · Kinkaku-ji · Tea Ceremony
⛩️
Record
Fushimi Inari has 10,000 torii gates — each donated by a business praying for profit
The seemingly infinite tunnel of vermillion torii gates winding up Mount Inari was not built by the government or the temple — each gate was donated by a Japanese business or individual, with their name inscribed on the back. The tradition began in the Edo period: the god Inari is the patron of commerce, foxes, rice, and fertility. Gates cost between ¥400,000 and ¥1,000,000 depending on size. Going at 6:30am as planned, you'll see the gates in mist with almost nobody else there — one of the great Japan experiences.
☕
Culture
The Japanese tea ceremony takes years to master — and nothing about it is casual
Chado ("the way of tea") was codified by the tea master Sen no Rikyu in the 16th century and elevated into a complete philosophy. Every movement in a tea ceremony — how the bowl is rotated, how the whisk is held, the direction you bow — carries meaning rooted in Zen Buddhism. The ceremony you'll attend today distills four principles: harmony (wa), respect (kei), purity (sei), and tranquility (jaku). Sen no Rikyu was so influential that he was eventually ordered to commit ritual suicide by the shogun — apparently for being too powerful.
🏯
History
Kinkaku-ji (the Golden Pavilion) was burned down by a student monk in 1950
The original 14th-century Golden Pavilion was destroyed in 1950 by a 22-year-old novice monk named Hayashi Yoken, who set fire to it and then attempted suicide on the grounds. He survived and was convicted of arson. The incident so disturbed Japan that author Yukio Mishima wrote a novel about it — "The Temple of the Golden Pavilion" (1956) — exploring the monk's obsession with beauty and destruction. The pavilion you'll see today is a 1955 reconstruction, now covered in five times more gold leaf than the original.
Jul 19 — Kyoto → Tokyo
🚅 Shinkansen return · Shibuya Sky
🚥
Record
Shibuya Crossing is the busiest pedestrian intersection in the world
Up to 3,000 people cross Shibuya Scramble during a single green light cycle at peak times — roughly 500,000 people a day. When the lights change, traffic stops in all directions simultaneously and pedestrians cross from every angle at once. The choreography is entirely self-organizing: no barriers, no marshals, almost never a collision. From Shibuya Sky above it, the crossing looks like a living organism — a perfect demonstration of Japan's social coordination operating at scale.
⛩️
History
Yasaka Shrine has been operating continuously for 1,350 years
Founded in 656 AD, Yasaka Shrine in Gion has survived earthquakes, fires, wars, and the complete transformation of the city around it. It is the origin point of the Gion Matsuri festival you attended the past two nights — all floats in the parade begin and end at Yasaka. The shrine is unusual in being open 24 hours, and its lanterns illuminate the Maruyama Park behind it around the clock. It's the most atmospheric spot in Kyoto in the morning before the crowds arrive.
🎸
Culture
Shibuya is where Japanese youth culture is invented — and exported to the world
The distinctive street fashion styles that have defined global youth culture — from Harajuku Lolita to Visual Kei to techwear — largely originate in the blocks around Shibuya and Harajuku. Japan's youth culture moves so fast that trends cycle in weeks rather than seasons. The area around Shibuya 109 department store has been ground zero for "gyaru" fashion, skatewear, and streetwear movements that later spread to Europe and the US. You're walking through the world's fastest-moving cultural laboratory.
Jul 20 — Tokyo Day 1
🏙️ Harajuku · Akihabara · Asakusa
⛩️
History
Senso-ji in Asakusa is Tokyo's oldest temple — and its founding legend involves fishermen and a golden statue
According to legend, in 628 AD two fishermen brothers pulled a tiny golden statue of the Bodhisattva Kannon from the Sumida River in their nets. Their village headman recognized its sacred nature and converted his home into a shrine to house it. That shrine became Senso-ji — now visited by 30 million people a year. The original statue has never been publicly displayed and remains hidden inside the inner sanctum to this day. Nobody alive has ever seen it.
🎮
Culture
Akihabara was a black market for electrical parts after WWII — and accidentally invented Japan's tech culture
After WWII, Akihabara became a chaotic street market where vendors sold surplus American electrical components. By the 1980s it had evolved into Japan's electronics hub, then into the center of anime, manga, and gaming culture as the same engineering-obsessed demographic drove both markets. The "otaku" culture born in Akihabara is now a $24 billion global industry. The maid cafes, figure shops, and multi-floor arcades you'll see today are the commercial manifestation of Japan's most powerful cultural export since sushi.
👗
Only in Japan
Harajuku's Takeshita Street is only 350 metres long — but shaped global fashion
The street you'll walk down is barely a third of a kilometre. Yet it's where Decora, Lolita, Visual Kei, and dozens of other Japanese street fashion movements were born. Fashion designer Rei Kawakubo (Comme des Garçons), Yohji Yamamoto, and Issey Miyake — three of the most influential designers of the last 50 years — all trace their aesthetic roots to this neighborhood. It famously inspired a Gwen Stefani album. Its creative energy comes from Tokyo's teenage population treating fashion as radical self-expression rather than commerce.
Jul 21 — Tokyo Day 2
🏙️ Tsukiji · TeamLab Planets · Borderless · JDM
🐟
Record
A single bluefin tuna at Tsukiji once sold for $3.1 million
In January 2019, a 278kg bluefin tuna sold at the first auction of the year for a record ¥333 million ($3.1 million) — roughly $11,000 per kilogram. The buyer, sushi restaurant chain Onodera Group, did it partly for publicity: the first auction of the new year is a prestige event where top buyers compete on price as a statement of ambition. The tuna you'll eat for breakfast cost significantly less. The outer market you're visiting is a retail extension of what was once the world's largest fish market, handling 700 tonnes of seafood daily.
🎨
Culture
TeamLab Planets holds a Guinness World Record — the most visited museum of a single art group
TeamLab — the art collective behind the experience you're visiting at 11:30am — is a group of over 1,000 ultra-technologists: artists, programmers, engineers, CG animators, and mathematicians who work collectively with no defined hierarchy. Their work has been called "the first truly digital art form." TeamLab Planets set the Guinness World Record for most visited museum by a single art group, attracting over 2.5 million visitors in one year. Remember: it's barefoot and involves wading through water — wear easy shoes.
🚗
Culture
Japan's car culture produced some of the most legendary machines in automotive history
Daikoku PA — the parking area you'll visit tonight — is where the JDM (Japanese Domestic Market) car culture was born. Cars like the Nissan Skyline GT-R, Toyota Supra, Honda NSX, and Mazda RX-7 were designed and sold exclusively in Japan before becoming globally legendary (often through illegal import and video games like Gran Turismo and Fast & Furious). On weekend nights, owners bring their builds to Daikoku to meet, show, and compare. It's essentially an open-air automotive museum — except everything is running and the owners want to talk about it.
Jul 22 — Tokyo Day 3
🏙️ Sumo · Shinjuku · Shimokitazawa · Golden Gai
🤼
History
Sumo is a 1,500-year-old Shinto ritual — not just a sport
Sumo wrestling originated as a ritual performed before the gods to pray for a good harvest. The ring (dohyo) is consecrated ground — a Shinto priest blesses it before every tournament and women are traditionally prohibited from entering it (a rule that sparked national controversy in 2018 when a female mayor attempted to enter to help an injured referee). The salt thrown before each bout is a purification ritual. A professional wrestler's entire life — diet, clothing, living arrangements, social hierarchy — is governed by the sport's strict traditions. The morning practice (keiko) you're attending is the most authentic way to experience it.
🎵
Culture
Shimokitazawa is Tokyo's answer to Brooklyn — and it nearly got destroyed by a highway
Shimokitazawa's dense network of vintage shops, tiny live music venues, and independent theatres developed in the 1960s and 70s around its two train stations. In the 2000s, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government planned to demolish much of the neighborhood to build a major surface road. Residents launched an extraordinary decade-long protest that eventually forced the road underground, preserving the neighborhood's character. The streets you'll wander this afternoon exist because of one of Japan's most successful grassroots urban campaigns.
🍺
Only in Japan
Golden Gai's 200 bars survived Tokyo's entire post-war redevelopment — through sheer stubbornness
Golden Gai was a black market district after WWII, then became associated with writers, artists, and filmmakers in the 1960s. When Tokyo's massive urban renewal programs swept through Shinjuku in the 1980s and 90s, developers repeatedly tried to buy out the tiny bar owners to build modern towers. The owners refused. Multiple times. Yakuza intimidation, arson attempts, and legal pressure failed to move them. The result: 200 bars that collectively seat perhaps 1,000 people, in an area the size of a supermarket car park, on some of the world's most valuable urban land.

History
Haneda Airport is older than Japan's commercial aviation industry
Haneda opened in 1931 as a tiny grass airstrip when Tokyo Bay was still open tidal flat. It was commandeered by the US military after WWII and returned to Japan in 1952. For decades it was Tokyo's only airport and handled all domestic and international flights. When Narita opened in 1978 (after one of Japan's most violent land protest movements, with farmers fighting riot police for years), Haneda became domestic-only. Its new international terminal, Terminal 3, opened in 2010 — the one you're departing from tonight.
🎁
Culture
The Japanese concept of "omiyage" means you must bring gifts from every trip — for everyone
Omiyage (お土産) is the Japanese tradition of bringing back local food gifts for colleagues, neighbors, and family members after any trip. It's not optional — it's a social obligation so deeply ingrained that every airport, train station, and tourist site in Japan sells beautifully packaged regional specialties specifically for this purpose. Haneda Terminal 3 has an exceptional Edo Market for exactly this. Budget at least 30 minutes and ¥5,000–10,000 per person. You cannot leave Japan without omiyage. The people back home are counting on you.
🌏
Culture
Japan has a word for "the bittersweet feeling of a journey ending" — Mono no Aware
Mono no aware (物の哀れ) — literally "the pathos of things" — is a Japanese aesthetic concept describing the gentle sadness of transience. It's the feeling you get watching cherry blossoms fall, or watching a place you love disappear in an airport window. The concept is central to Japanese art, poetry, and philosophy — the idea that beauty is inseparable from its impermanence. As your flight lifts off from Haneda tonight, you'll probably understand exactly what it means.
